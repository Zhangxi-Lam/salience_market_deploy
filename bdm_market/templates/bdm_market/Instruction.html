Basic Idea

In the main part of the experiment, you will play 40 trading periods of an investment game. In each trading period, you will simultanously trade {{ num_assets }} risky asset(s) (labelled from A to Z, depending on the number of assets) with a computer investor. The return of the assets depends on the state of the assets. There are {{ num_states }} possible states. At the beginning each trading period, you are informed of the number of assets, the number of all possible states, the probability of each state, and the return of the assets at each state.

{% if state_independent %}

During each trading period, you trade assets in the game but do NOT know the true state of the assets. The state of each asset is drawn independently. That is, it is possible that the assets have different states. At the end of the trading period, the system tosses a 10-sided dice FOR EACH ASSET, and randomly assigns the true states of the assets depending on the probability of each state. Your payoff in each trading period depends on the number of each type of assets you own and the true state of the assets.

For example, suppose that there are two states: state 1 with probability 40% and state 2 with probability 60%. You do NOT know which state will become the true state during the trading period. At the end of the trading period, the true state of each asset will be randomly determined based on the probabilities of the two states. Since the computer uses a 10-sided fair dice, then throwing 1-4 means state 1 is chosen, while throwing 5-10 means state 2 is chosen. If there are two assets, it is possible that Asset A is in state 1 and Asset B is in state 2.

During each trading period, you trade assets in the market but do NOT know the true state of the assets. The assets share the same state. At the end of the trading period, the system tosses a 10-sided dice, and randomly assigns the true state of the assets depending on the probability of each state. How to Trade

At the beginning of the trading period, you are endowed with some cash and some assets. FOR EACH ASSET, you need to set up a bid (the price you are willing to buy) and an ask (the price you are willing to sell). Your bid should not be higher than your ask, since you do not want to buy with a high price but sell with a low price. The computer investor then comes to the market with a random price for each asset you trade. The random price is a random integer between the maximum possible and the minimum possible return of the assets. The actual trade depends on the investor's random price, and your bid and ask.

For example, the investor comes with a price X for Asset A.

  • If X is not higher than your bid, then you will buy 1 unit of Asset A from the investor.
  • If X is not lower than your ask, then you will sell 1 unit of your Asset A
  • If X is between your bid and ask, no trade will happen.
The trade for other assets work in the same manner.

Payment

At the end of the trading period, you will see a result page that summarizes your actions and performance. The result page reveals the true state of the assets, your final asset holding, your choices, and your payoff. Your total payoff will be your remaining cash + your earning from your final asset holding. You can think of the cash as a risk-free asset which provides you a guaranteed 1:1 return, while the tradable assets are risky.

After the result page, all traders go on together to the next trading period.

At the end of the experiment, one non-practice trading period will be randomly chosen by the system, and your final payoff from this experiment is your payoff at that trading period (after converted to US dollar), plus your show-up fee. So your objective is to try to maximize your payoff at every non-practice trading period.

If you are ready for the experiment, please raise your hand. The experiment starts when everyone is ready.
